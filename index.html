Meet Staphylococcus aureus
Staphylococcus aureus is a bacterium that lives on human skin and in nasal passages. It's usually harmless — but if it gets into a wound or the bloodstream, it can cause serious infection.

Like all bacteria, Staph is a prokaryote — no true nucleus, just a circular chromosome floating in the cytoplasm. It has pili (hair-like projections) and reproduces by binary fission — similar to the cell division you studied in the cell cycle unit, but simpler: one cell splits directly into two identical daughters with no mitotic spindle.

In 1945, penicillin became widely available. It was a miracle drug. But Staph had a trick.

A Random Copying Error
Every time a bacterium divides, it copies its entire chromosome. Staphylococcus divides every 20–30 minutes — that's up to 72 copies per day per cell.

Sometimes the copy contains a random error — a mutation. Most mutations are harmful or neutral. But occasionally, a mutation changes a protein in a way that happens to block penicillin from working.

The bacterium didn't "try" to become resistant. The mutation was random. Penicillin didn't cause it. It just happened — and then penicillin made it matter.
Key idea: Mutations happen by chance during DNA copying. They are random — not directed by the environment. Natural selection acts on mutations that already exist.

Cell Division: Binary Fission and the Cell Cycle
Bacteria reproduce by binary fission — you can think of it as a stripped-down version of the cell cycle you already know. The parent cell duplicates its DNA (like S phase), then pinches into two identical daughter cells (like cytokinesis) — but without the mitotic spindle. The parent is gone. Both daughters carry the exact same DNA.

This means: if a bacterium has a resistance mutation, both daughters inherit it. If it doesn't, neither will. There's no mixing of genes between parents like in sexual reproduction.

This is why resistance spreads so fast — one resistant cell can become billions in 24 hours, all carrying the same resistance gene.
Key idea: Binary fission is asexual reproduction — like a simplified cell cycle. Offspring are exact clones of the parent. Resistance alleles are inherited with perfect fidelity every division. They can only spread, never dilute.
Slide 4 of 6
Penicillin Kills — But Not All
Penicillin works by blocking a protein that bacteria use to build their cell wall. Without a cell wall, the bacterium bursts and dies.

The resistance mutation changes that protein slightly, so penicillin can't bind to it. The resistant bacterium builds its wall normally and survives.

Partially resistant bacteria have one normal copy and one mutated copy of the gene. They get partial protection — sometimes enough, sometimes not.
Key idea: Penicillin is the selective pressure. It removes susceptible bacteria from the population. Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce — passing their advantage to all offspring.
Slide 5 of 6
Resistance Allele Frequency Increases Each Generation
Each time penicillin is applied, susceptible bacteria die and resistant ones survive. After reproducing, the proportion of resistant bacteria increases.

The bars below show this shift. This is what scientists mean by "allele frequency" — the fraction of the population carrying a particular version of a gene.

Notice that the resistance bar only ever grows. That's the hallmark of directional natural selection — one phenotype is consistently favored.
Key idea: Natural selection changes allele frequencies over generations. With antibiotic pressure, the resistance allele frequency increases every generation — it can never go backwards.
